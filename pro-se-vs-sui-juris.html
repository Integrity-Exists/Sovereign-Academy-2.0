Pro Se vs. Sui Juris: What’s the Difference?

If you're representing yourself in court, you may see two terms: Pro Se and Sui Juris. They are NOT the same — and how you present yourself can affect how you're treated in court.

⚖️ Pro Se

Latin for "on one’s own behalf." Courts recognize this as a standard legal status for someone acting without an attorney. But you're still considered a “legal person” under the court's corporate jurisdiction.

👁️‍🗨️ Sui Juris

Latin for "of one’s own right." This implies you're appearing as a natural man or woman under common law, not under the full jurisdiction of the court’s corporate/legal fiction. It signals sovereignty — and often resistance to standard assumptions.

🧠 Which One Should I Use?

If you're aiming to exercise rights beyond normal procedure (like challenging jurisdiction, or asserting constitutional status), you may prefer to appear Sui Juris. But be ready — some judges don’t like it, and you'll need to speak with precision and strength.
